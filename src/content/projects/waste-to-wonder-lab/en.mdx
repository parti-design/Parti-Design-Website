Waste to Wonder Lab began with a simple but provocative question: what if the things a city throws away could become the things people gather around? Commissioned by Umeå Kommun for Umecom Fest, the project explored local waste flows and transformed them into a public installation for Broparken as part of the city's 400th birthday celebrations.

Following the design sprint methodology, Parti Design worked with local youth to research, collect, test, and build with discarded materials. The result was an octopus-inspired family of public furniture elements that invited people to sit, play, and see waste differently.

## The Brief

As part of Umecom Fest and Umeå's 400th birthday, Parti Design was asked to investigate the city's waste streams and turn them into something generous, playful, and public. The brief was not only about making objects. It was about changing the story around waste itself, from something hidden and discarded into something visible, useful, and full of possibility.

## From Waste Stream to Workshop

The project followed a compressed, hands-on co-design process that moved quickly from research into making. Local youth were central throughout. They mapped where waste comes from, learned how materials move through the city, and helped shape what the final installation could become.

Working out of Mötesplats Stöcke, the team sourced materials from local waste streams and developed a modular furniture system inspired by Umecom's octopus mascot. The process was educational and practical at the same time, linking circular thinking to direct physical experience.

## An Octopus in Broparken

The final installation took the form of an interactive octopus. At its centre was a tensegrity sphere, with tentacle-like elements stretching outward into different uses and scales. One became a beanbag. Another became a table. Another became a crawl-through tube for children. Each piece had its own character, but together they read as one playful landscape built entirely from waste.

Placed in Broparken during Umecom Fest, the installation immediately drew people in. Children climbed and played. Adults stopped, sat down, and looked more closely. The project worked as public furniture, but it also worked as a conversation starter: what counts as waste, and what new value can be created when we look at local material flows differently?

## More Than an Installation

Waste to Wonder Lab was one of the highlights of the festival, but its deeper value was in the process behind it. It demonstrated how co-design, circular material thinking, and public space experimentation can come together in a format that is accessible, educational, and joyful.

The project remains a strong example of Parti Design's approach: research turned into making, participation turned into ownership, and overlooked local resources turned into shared public value.
