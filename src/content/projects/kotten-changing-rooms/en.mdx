Kotten Changing Rooms began as a practical need. Kotten Sauna had quickly become a beloved gathering point for Umeå Kallbad, and the next step was clear: the site needed an extension that could support the growing community without losing the spirit that had made the sauna so special in the first place.

Parti Design was brought in to design that extension and to facilitate the co-building process around it. The proposal took its cues from the language of Kotten Sauna itself, carrying forward the same character while placing special emphasis on modularity and movability. The result was not just an addition, but a structure designed to stay flexible, practical, and rooted in the evolving life of the place.

## Built in the Cold, Built Together

Construction took place over two months, through January and February, in the kind of cold that makes even simple tasks feel hard. Temperatures hovered around -18 C. Still, week after week, people showed up. The build was organised as regular Community Build Days, with roughly 10 to 20 participants joining each session to help bring the extension into being.

The process was carefully structured so that volunteers could contribute meaningfully. The work was broken into stages and manageable tasks, while more complex parts were pre-measured and cut in advance. That balance mattered. It meant participants could step into the build with confidence, do real work, and feel that their effort had immediate value.

## From Strangers to Regulars

What stands out most is not only that the building got built, but what happened around it. Even after a long day in brutal winter weather, people left smiling, tired, and grateful to have taken part. The build days offered something rare: a free, hands-on experience that felt generous, social, and deeply worthwhile.

Many of the volunteers arrived as strangers. Over the course of the project, a number of them became regular members of the community and joined in other shared activities around Umeå Kallbad. In that sense, the changing rooms were not only constructed by the community. They helped construct the community too.

## A Case for Regenerative Placemaking

By running full-day sessions and including lunch, the project became more than a building process. It became a social ritual. A place where work, conversation, learning, and belonging could happen at the same time. That is the deeper lesson of the project: when people help shape a place with their own hands, they build a sense of connection to it that cannot be designed in afterwards.

Kotten Changing Rooms is a strong case study in community-driven development. It shows what becomes possible when local spaces are shaped through participatory processes that build community, culture, and identity alongside physical structures. It is regenerative placemaking, participatory design, and innovation in practice.
