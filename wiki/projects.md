### Kotten Sauna

**Client:** Umeå Kallbad
**Location:** Bölesholmerna, Umeå
**Year:** 2025
**Status:** Completed
**Services:** Architecture & Spatial Design, Co-design Workshops, Placemaking Consulting
**Collaborators:** Sebastian Ulonska, Jon Krebs, Garei Gattaulin (Core Team)
**CMS slug:** `kotten-sauna`

**Tagline:** Umeå's first community-built sauna — shaped by pinecones, reclaimed pallets, and fifty volunteers.

**Description:**
Kotten is the first sauna of Umeå Kallbad — built over one year through community-powered Sunday build days. Its name and form are drawn from the Swedish word for pinecone, and its facade is clad entirely in hand-cut shingles made from reclaimed pallet wood, each one shaped by the hands of over fifty volunteers. It is a small building with a big story: proof that the way you build something can be just as meaningful as what you build.

**Full write-up:**

*From Fika Wagon to Sauna*
The journey began with an unlikely starting point — an old fika wagon used by construction workers on a building site. Umeå Kallbad had a vision and a growing community behind them, but no permanent location and no guarantee of a permit. What they did have was momentum: a grassroots crowdfunding campaign that raised 65,000 SEK from over 100 backers through nothing more than word of mouth and social media. That early surge of support made one thing unmistakably clear — there was real appetite for this.

*Co-Building as Community Strategy*
The decision to build with volunteers wasn't simply a matter of budget — it was a deliberate strategy. Every Sunday for a year, a group of enthusiasts gathered at the site to work on the sauna, share a meal, and get to know each other. These weren't just build days; they were the founding moments of a community. The shared labour forged bonds and a collective sense of ownership that no marketing campaign could replicate. Parti Design facilitated the design process in close collaboration with the core team and the volunteers themselves. The design emerged from dialogue, shaped over time by the same people who would build and use it.

*A Design Born from Nature*
Kotten takes its name — and its silhouette — from the pinecone. The overlapping shingle cladding was drawn directly from this reference, each layer echoing the scales of the cone in miniature. The shingles were made entirely from reclaimed pallet wood: pallets were broken apart by hand, cut to size, and fixed one by one across the facade. It is the kind of labour-intensive detail that would be uneconomical in conventional construction, but is perfectly suited to volunteer effort — repetitive, accessible, and deeply satisfying to contribute to. Natural wood fibre insulation completes the material palette, keeping the building warm, breathable, and rooted in its forest-adjacent setting.

*One Winter to Prove It*
With a single-season trial permit granted at Bölesholmerna, Kotten opened its doors for its first winter. Each Sunday, hundreds of people joined free community cold-bathing sessions — a ritual that quickly became a fixture of Umeå life. The season was an unqualified success. On the strength of it, the Kommun granted a permanent building permit, and Kotten became a lasting feature of the city's public landscape.

*The Story Is the Project*
Umeå Kallbad understood early that the story of how a community space is made is just as powerful as the space itself. The shingles carry the fingerprints of fifty volunteers. The timber holds the memory of a hundred Sunday mornings. And the warmth inside is not just from the stove — it is the accumulated energy of a community that built something together, and keeps coming back because of it. That story now lives in the DNA of Umeå Kallbad. Kotten is its first, most tangible expression.

### Connect Tviste

**Client:** Kajeka / Campus X
**Location:** Tvistevägen, Umeå, Sweden
**Year:** 2024
**Status:** Study / Concept
**Services:** UX/UI & Digital, Co-design Workshops, Facilitation & Project Management
**Collaborators:** Kasimir Suter Winter, Lisa Selin, Zoé Magnusson, Jonas Eltes
**CMS slug:** `connect-tviste`

**Tagline:** Belonging through a connected neighbourhood

**Description:**
Connect Tviste was a prototype week project exploring how to create a greater sense of belonging at Tviste — a student housing area in Umeå run by Kajeka's Campus X. Through resident interviews and user journey mapping, we identified that students moved into an area without community infrastructure: no welcoming contact, no indoor gathering spaces, and no shared ownership of the neighbourhood. Working across service design, spatial thinking, and digital product, the team designed a connected system of three interventions — a repurposed community building (Tvistehuset), a paid resident advisor role (Husvärd), and a redesigned digital platform — all tied together through a resident-led student organisation called Tviste Club with its own mini-grant fund.

**Full write-up:**

*The Problem*
Students arriving at Tviste found themselves in a neighbourhood without community. No one welcomed them. There were no indoor places to gather. Resources like bikes and tools sat wasted. Spontaneous socialising only happened in summer, when the sun brought people outside. The result: low ownership, low safety, and a high churn of disconnected residents. Key insights from our research pointed to three missing ingredients: ownership, agency, and a sense of home.

*Tvistehuset — A community club house*
We proposed repurposing an existing building at the heart of Tviste into a community hub. The programme includes a social space, an info desk, toilets, and a tools and repair studio. Two adjacent bike sheds are converted into Tvistebanken — a shared resource storage managed by residents — with summer storage and smart object detection for security.

*Husvärd — Resident Advisors*
Kajeka hires existing residents as paid community activators working part time. Husvärdar welcome new students, manage Tvistebanken, organise events, and coordinate student initiatives. They work in rotating groups across three responsibilities: Tvistebanken, Mini Grants, and Tvistehuset.

*Campus X Platform — A redesigned digital home*
We redesigned the Campus X website to serve as the connective tissue between students and the neighbourhood. A new Services section partners with Umeå Energi, LF Västerbotten, Umeå Kommun, and Umeå Universitet to help students navigate moving in, home safety, internet setup, and student health. A dedicated Tviste Club section surfaces community events and the mini-grant programme.

*Tviste Club — Community with a budget*
Tviste Club is a student organisation hosted on Open Collective through Massvis, giving residents a transparent, manageable budget for Tvistehuset. A mini-grants flow lets any resident apply for funding — applications reviewed and approved by the Husvärd, receipts paid out by Massvis. This gives students real financial agency to activate their own neighbourhood.

*Graphic Profile*
As part of the prototype we also developed a graphic identity for the Tviste area — earthy reds and forest greens, with Be Vietnam Pro for headings and Roboto for body text.

### Waste to Wonder Lab

**Client:** Umeå Kommun
**Location:** Broparken, Umeå, Sweden
**Year:** 2022
**Status:** Completed
**Services:** Architecture & Spatial Design, Co-design Workshops, Research & Development
**Collaborators:** Karina Suter Winter, Garei Gataullin, Elin Holmberg, Adele Valtersson, Roger Filipsson, Innovation X, Mötesplats Stöcke
**CMS slug:** `waste-to-wonder-lab`
**Editorial note (2026-03-12):** Keep the project under the slug `waste-to-wonder-lab` in both locales.

**Tagline:** Turning Umeå's waste flows into public furniture for the city's 400th birthday

**Description:**
Waste to Wonder Lab was an exploratory project researching waste flows in and around Umeå, then transforming that waste into public space furniture for Broparken as part of Umecom Fest — a celebration of Umeå's 400th birthday. Following the design sprint methodology, the project engaged local youth in developing the design, collecting and learning about waste, and building the final pieces together. The outcome was an interactive installation inspired by the octopus — the logo of Umecom. A central ball built from a tensegrity structure anchored a set of tentacles, each a separate element with a different function: a beanbag, a table, a tube for children to play in. A playful, functional, and thought-provoking collection of objects made entirely from waste.

**Full write-up:**

*The Brief*
As part of Umecom Fest and Umeå's 400th birthday celebrations, Parti Design was commissioned by Umeå Kommun to explore the city's waste flows — and turn them into something wonderful.

*Process*
The project followed the design sprint methodology, a compressed, hands-on approach to co-design that brings participants from research to finished object in a short timeframe. Local youth were central throughout: mapping waste, understanding where it comes from and where it ends up, contributing to the design, and helping build the final pieces. The team worked out of Mötesplats Stöcke, sourcing materials from local waste streams and developing a modular furniture system inspired by the Umecom mascot.

*The Installation*
The result was an interactive octopus-inspired installation in Broparken. A central tensegrity ball formed the body, with tentacles radiating outward — each one a distinct functional object. A beanbag. A table. A tube for children to crawl through and play in. Every element made from collected local waste. The installation attracted children and adults alike, inviting people to sit, play, and reflect: what is waste? How does something become waste? And how can it be transformed back into wonder?

*Outcome*
The project was a highlight of Umecom Fest, drawing crowds throughout the event and sparking conversations about circular materials, urban waste, and community-led making.

### Rewilding Sweden — Pavilion

**Client:** Rewilding Sweden
**Location:** Sweden
**Year:** 2023
**Status:** Completed
**Services:** Architecture & Spatial Design, Research & Development
**CMS slug:** `rewilding-sweden-pavilion`

**Tagline:** A geodesic dome pavilion designed and built for Rewilding Sweden — structural geometry meets ecological storytelling.

**Description:**
Rewilding Sweden needed a temporary pavilion for their annual gathering — a space that could host talks, workshops, and exhibitions while communicating their ecological mission. Parti Design proposed a geodesic dome: structurally efficient, visually striking, and entirely demountable. The dome was designed to be assembled and disassembled by a small team without specialist tools. It was used as a central gathering and exhibition space, housing displays about rewilding ecology and hosting the event's programme. The project demonstrates how temporary architecture can be both high-quality and low-impact — built from standard components, leaving no trace when the event is over.

### DIT Egnahem

**Type:** Architecture, Research & Development
**Location:** Umeå, Sweden
**Year:** 2023
**Status:** In development
**CMS slug:** `dit-egnahem`

**Tagline:** The first WikiHouse in Sweden — do-it-together self-build housing for communities.

**Description:**
WikiHouse is an open-source, CNC-cut timber building system — designed so that anyone can download the plans, cut the parts, and assemble a structurally sound building without specialist construction knowledge. DIT Egnahem adapted this system for Swedish climate, planning conditions, and the byggemenskap (community self-build) model. The structure was cut from standard sheet materials at a local CNC workshop and assembled on site in a weekend. The timber frame slots together like a giant puzzle — no nails, no heavy machinery, no expert labour required. The completed building demonstrates that high-quality, architecturally considered housing can be built by communities themselves, at a fraction of the cost of conventional construction. It is a proof of concept for a new model of housing in Sweden.

**Long-term potential:** This venture is the foundation of Parti Design's byggemenskap offer. A successful DIT Egnahem creates a replicable model for community self-build housing across Sweden.

### Umeå Kallbad

**Type:** Co-design Workshops, Facilitation & Project Management, Architecture & Spatial Design
**Location:** Umeå, Sweden
**Year:** Ongoing
**Status:** Active
**CMS slug:** `umea-kallbad`

**Tagline:** A community-led kallbadhus and cold water bathing initiative in Umeå.

**Description:**
Umeå had been talking about a public bathing facility for years — and no public budget had materialised. Rather than waiting, Parti Design took it on as a community venture: forming a group of residents, applying for grants, and running the design and construction as a series of co-building workshops. The sauna and changing room were designed collaboratively. Participants made decisions about layout, materials, and character. The structure was then built over two seasons of weekend workshops, with community members handling the construction under Parti Design's guidance. The facility opened in 2024 and is now a gathering point for cold water swimming throughout the year — from midsummer dips to midwinter ice bathing.

**Kotten Sauna** is the first completed structure of this venture. See the Kotten Sauna project entry for the full story.
**Editorial note (2026-03-12):** The portfolio project now lives under the slug `kotten-changing-rooms` and is titled **Kotten Changing Rooms** to distinguish it from the Umeå Kallbad venture. It documents the modular changing room extension to Kotten Sauna and the two-month winter co-building process facilitated by Parti Design, and the copy should emphasize the social transformation from volunteer build days into lasting community.
